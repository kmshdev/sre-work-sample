01

7 Day Execution Plan

Keep the implementation small enough that health states, alerts, recovery, and documentation stay coherent. Build the smallest local runtime-fleet slice that proves your operational model, then write down tradeoffs before expanding scope.

Day 1

Scope the platform and define health

Pick a stack, sketch the control service, three runtimes, one mock dependency, and the operator surface. Define runtime identity and the health-state table before writing much code.

architecture runtime identity health states
Day 2

Add observability and failure injection

Expose health and metrics, add searchable logs, define at least three alert rules, and start proving two operational failures plus release safety.

metrics alerts failure injection
Day 3

Write runbooks and collect evidence

Document deployment, rollout, rollback, operator controls, security assumptions, and recovery paths. Complete NO_AGENT_INCIDENT.md without coding agents or LLMs.

runbooks security evidence
Days 4-7

Simplify, validate, and prepare the report

Tighten anything that became too broad. Ensure a reviewer can run the project from a fresh checkout and understand what is implemented, simulated, or omitted.

validation submission report tradeoffs
02

Pass The Filter

This take-home decides whether we should spend interview time with you. It is not a final hiring decision, and it is not scored by feature count.

Hard gates

  • Your submission runs from a fresh checkout with exact setup and smoke commands.
  • Your delivery automation evidence shows validation, promotion, and rollback intent.
  • Your health model separates liveness from operational eligibility.
  • Two operational failures include trigger, detection, state change, alert path, and recovery.
  • Your release-safety scenario is implemented or documented with validation and rollback intent.
  • Your NO_AGENT_INCIDENT.md is credible and clearly no-agent work.
  • Your solution respects the no real systems, no secrets, and no paid-review constraints.

Usually fails

  • Treating /healthz == 200 as the health model.
  • Submitting alerts without thresholds, severity, owner, or action.
  • Using restart-only recovery as the whole incident response.
  • Providing broad AI-generated prose without specific evidence.
  • Missing DEFENSE_NOTES.md, NO_AGENT_INCIDENT.md, or AI usage disclosure.
03

Do / Avoid

The assignment is not scored by feature count. It is scored by whether your design would be understandable and safe to operate under partial failure.

Do

  • Prove clear health states and what each state allows or blocks.
  • Show alerts that are actionable, with page versus ticket severity.
  • Include exact run commands and a short smoke-test path.
  • Demonstrate two operational failure scenarios with real evidence.
  • Separate implemented evidence from planned-but-not-implemented scenarios.

Avoid

  • Do not connect to real brokers, exchanges, customer systems, or secrets.
  • Do not require paid infrastructure for review.
  • Do not treat restart-only behavior as a complete recovery model.
  • Do not spend most of the time on UI polish while observability is thin.
  • Do not hide AI-generated work; document how you verified it.

Deliverables Checklist

Use this as a final pass before submission. Every item should be easy to find from your README or submission report.

05

Evidence to Capture

Evidence beats claims. Prefer small, inspectable artifacts that a reviewer can rerun locally or understand quickly in a follow-up interview.

Run evidence

  • Fresh checkout setup commands and expected output.
  • Smoke-test command that proves the control surface, runtimes, mock dependency, and health response.
  • Delivery automation evidence such as workflow output, local dry run, or transcript.
  • Health endpoint or CLI output for states exercised by your implemented slice.
  • Metrics scrape, dashboard export, or representative metric samples.

Failure evidence

  • Command or switch used to trigger each required failure.
  • Log excerpt showing detection and state transition.
  • Alert rule or monitor definition with expected severity.
  • Recovery command and proof that the system returned to the intended state.
  • Clear labels for implemented scenarios versus documented-only plans.
Keep screenshots useful. A screenshot should show state, timestamps, runtime identity, and what action is blocked or allowed. A pretty chart without operational context is weak evidence.
06

No-Agent Incident Segment

Complete NO_AGENT_INCIDENT.md without coding agents or LLMs. Normal documentation, shell tools, and your own notes are allowed.

What strong answers include

  • First five checks that distinguish control-surface reachability from data freshness failure.
  • Severity choice with a clear reason and initial blast-radius estimate.
  • Explicit decision about pausing affected runtimes or blocking unsafe actions.
  • Who gets paged or notified and what operators should be told.
  • First runbook action and the evidence needed to close or downgrade.
07

Self-Review Rubric

Before submitting, read your own repo as if you were the on-call engineer receiving it during an incident.

Reliability

Judgment under ambiguity

Tradeoffs are explicit, scope is controlled, and unsafe or unknown states fail closed.

Health

State correctness

Each state has entry signals, allowed actions, alert behavior, and recovery guidance.

Observability

Actionable signals

Metrics, logs, health checks, and alerts explain both impact and next action.

Operations

Runbook clarity

A reviewer can start, break, inspect, and recover the system from documented steps.

Deployment

20-50 runtime realism

Isolation, identity, secrets, rollout rings, and rollback criteria are practical.

Tooling

AI transparency

Agentic tool usage is disclosed, verified, and balanced by no-agent reasoning.
