Evidence and validation guide

Proof beats explanation.

The work sample doesn't need production breadth. It does need proof that the local slice starts, fails visibly, blocks unsafe work, recovers, and can be inspected by another engineer.

Minimum evidence set

Capture evidence where the reviewer will look first.

Area Evidence Why it matters
Fresh checkout Setup, start, smoke, and cleanup commands with expected output. Shows the reviewer can reproduce the system.
Smoke command One command that checks control, runtimes, dependency, and health. Creates a fast review gate.
Failure scenarios Trigger, detection, state transition, alert path, recovery, and proof. Shows operational behavior under stress.
Delivery automation Workflow, Make target, Taskfile, or equivalent validation path. Shows reliability expectations are repeatable.
Release safety Bad config or bad rollout with validation and rollback thinking. Shows blast-radius and promotion judgment.

Evidence quality

Strong evidence is concrete and rerunnable.

Strong evidence

  • Commands include expected output or captured output.
  • Logs identify runtime, state, timestamp, and dependency.
  • Alerts include threshold, severity, owner, and action.
  • Reports separate implemented behavior from future plans.

Weak evidence

  • A screenshot without commands or scenario context.
  • A health endpoint that only proves the process is alive.
  • An alert name with no threshold or operator action.
  • AI-generated prose not tied to commands, logs, or tests.

Suggested artifact locations

Put proof where reviewers can find it.

  • SUBMISSION.md for the final report.
  • evidence/ for logs, screenshots, transcripts, and samples.
  • docs/OPERATIONS.md for runbooks and alert notes.
  • docs/ARCHITECTURE.md for system shape and tradeoffs.
  • DEFENSE_NOTES.md for follow-up interview preparation.
Evidence should let the reviewer inspect what happened and rerun what matters.
